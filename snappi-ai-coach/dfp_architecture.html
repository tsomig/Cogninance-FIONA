Theoretical Framework

ğŸ¯ Foundational Premise

Adult financial behaviors are significantly shaped by unconscious beliefs formed in childhood. By identifying these patterns and addressing them with developmentally-appropriate interventions, we can achieve behavior change that cognitive-only approaches cannot.

Three Pillars of DFP

ğŸ“œ

1. Money Scripts Theory (Klontz)

Core Insight: Unconscious beliefs about money, typically formed before age 10, drive adult financial behavior.

"Money scripts are typically unconscious, developed in childhood, passed down from generation to generation within families and cultures, contextually bound, and often only partial truths." â€” Klontz & Klontz, 2009

Four Script Categories:

  • Money Avoidance: "Money is bad/I don't deserve it"
  • Money Worship: "More money will solve everything"
  • Money Status: "Self-worth = Net worth"
  • Money Vigilance: "Money is shameful/secret"
ğŸ”—

2. Attachment Theory (Bowlby/Ainsworth)

Core Insight: Early caregiver relationships create internal working models that shape how we relate to security, risk, and resources.

"Money is 90% emotion, 10% logic... Attachment styles, the emotional blueprints we develop in childhood, profoundly influence our ability to manage budgets, save, and invest." â€” Brad Klontz

Four Attachment Styles:

  • Secure: Balanced financial approach
  • Anxious: Compulsive checking, fear of scarcity
  • Avoidant: Financial denial, ignoring money
  • Fearful-Avoidant: Oscillating extremes
ğŸ‘¶

3. Inner Child / Reparenting

Core Insight: Healing requires addressing the wounded child-self that formed the maladaptive pattern.

Application to Finance:

  • The "inner child" holds the original money wound
  • "Reparenting" = providing what wasn't given (safety, permission, validation)
  • Nudges can serve as micro-reparenting moments
  • Goal: Rewire the emotional relationship with money

Intervention Design: Inner Child Nudges

âš ï¸ Critical Design Principle

The goal is to heal, not manipulate. Every nudge should serve the user's authentic wellbeing, not engagement metrics or product sales. This is "financial reparenting," not "exploitation of childhood wounds."

Nudge Architecture

ğŸ¯

Tone Calibration

Different money scripts require different communication styles:

  • Avoidance: Gentle, non-judgmental, normalizing
  • Worship: Grounding, realistic, values-focused
  • Status: Self-worth affirming, identity-separating
  • Vigilance: Permission-giving, safety-affirming
â°

Timing Optimization

Deliver nudges at psychologically receptive moments:

  • Post-stress: After detected emotional spending
  • Pre-decision: Before large purchase patterns
  • Milestone: At FRI improvement points
  • Avoidance break: After prolonged app absence
ğŸ’¬

Reparenting Elements

Include what the inner child needed but didn't get:

  • Safety: "Your financial situation is manageable"
  • Permission: "It's okay to enjoy some of what you've earned"
  • Validation: "Many people feel this way about money"
  • Guidance: "Here's a small step that might help"

Ethical Guardrails

ğŸš¨ The Fundamental Tension

Speaking to someone's "inner child" can be profoundly healing OR deeply manipulative. The difference is intent and consent. Every design decision must be evaluated through this lens.

Ethical Framework: The Three Tests

1ï¸âƒ£

The Therapist Test

"Would a licensed financial therapist approve of this intervention?"

If the nudge would make a professional uncomfortable, don't send it.

2ï¸âƒ£

The Transparency Test

"If the user fully understood how we're using their psychology, would they still want this?"

If it only works when hidden, it's manipulation.

3ï¸âƒ£

The Outcome Test

"Does this serve the user's authentic wellbeing or our engagement metrics?"

If it increases app usage but not financial health, reconsider.

Mandatory Safeguards

ğŸ”

Explicit Consent Layer

Users must opt-in to "psychological insights" with clear explanation:

  • "Fiona can learn about your money mindset to give more personalized support"
  • "This includes understanding patterns from your childhood and emotions"
  • "You can turn this off anytime"

GDPR Note: Psychological profiling requires explicit consent under Art. 9 (special category data).

Trauma Threshold Detection

System monitors for signs that intervention is causing harm:

  • Negative sentiment spikes after DFP nudges
  • User explicitly references distress/trauma
  • Behavioral patterns suggesting mental health crisis

Action: Automatically de-escalate to supportive-only mode.

  • Provide warm handoff to financial therapy resources
  • "Some of what you're experiencing might benefit from deeper support. Here are some resources..."
